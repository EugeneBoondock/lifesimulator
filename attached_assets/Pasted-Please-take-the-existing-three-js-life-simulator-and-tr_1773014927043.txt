Please take the existing three.js life simulator and transform it into a deeply believable artificial life sandbox. Right now the characters feel amateur, stiff, ugly, low-effort, and disconnected from what they are supposedly doing. They mostly just stand around or play a basic walking animation, and that breaks the fantasy immediately. I do not want placeholder energy. I want this to feel like the early foundation of a true “Sims for AI species” sandbox, where autonomous beings visibly live, act, react, create, socialize, work, build, rest, breed, learn, and survive inside a coherent world.

This should not be treated like a tiny cosmetic pass. Treat it like a serious refactor of the whole character presentation and behavior expression layer. Go all out. This is meant to be a sandbox for artificial life itself. The world should feel alive even when the player is doing nothing.

The current visual result is not acceptable. The 3D models look too primitive, too cheap, too static, and too disconnected from the simulation state. I want the simulation to look like the agents are genuinely doing what their minds are deciding. If an agent is hungry, I want to see that hunger in posture, movement, target selection, object interaction, and eating behavior. If an agent is building, I want to see building motions, carrying behavior, placement behavior, maybe even brief inspection or correction behavior. If they are socializing, I want body orientation, gesture, pauses, response timing, and emotional posture changes. If they are tired, injured, scared, curious, attracted, territorial, proud, or playful, those inner states should visibly affect how they stand, move, and behave.

I want you to rebuild the character pipeline in a way that is scalable, modular, high-quality, and future-proof, while keeping it compatible with three.js. Use the proper tools in the three.js ecosystem. You may use GLTFLoader, DRACOLoader, AnimationMixer, and SkeletonUtils for asset loading, compression, rigged animation playback, and safe cloning of skinned meshes. Use InstancedMesh only for repeated environmental props, not for unique animated characters unless you intentionally build a custom animation path for that. If you need a strong asset pipeline, use Blender for authoring or cleaning models, and Mixamo for auto-rigging and starter animations if custom rigged assets are not ready yet. I want a professional path, not a pile of temporary hacks.

The art direction should stay stylized and fit the world. The current world has a soft low-poly charm, and I want the characters to match that. That means cozy, clean, readable, charming low-poly humanoids or species characters with attractive silhouettes, readable body language, and enough detail to feel intentional. I do not want random cubes glued together, stiff marionette-looking bodies, tiny puppet limbs, or low-effort mannequins. The characters should have proper structure: head, neck, torso, pelvis, upper and lower arms, hands, upper and lower legs, feet, and optional equipment slots such as backpack, tool, hat, hair, or accessories. Their proportions should be stylized but appealing: slightly bigger heads for personality, proper shoulders and hips so animations look grounded, readable hands and feet, and bodies that feel designed rather than accidentally assembled.

If there are no good character assets yet, build a stronger procedural fallback character system in code with actual limb hierarchy and joint-driven movement. Even the fallback should look good. It should not just be stacked primitive shapes. It should have thought behind silhouette, tapering limbs, posture presets, face design, and outfit variation. But the preferred direction is still a real .glb character pipeline with rigged meshes and animation clips.

Every character must support a real animation state machine. A walking loop alone is nowhere near enough. At minimum I want idle, look around, think, walk, hurry, gather, pick up, carry, eat, talk, wave, sit, sleep, inspect, celebrate, low-energy idle, sad idle, hurt or exhausted movement, interact with object, work, build, place item, craft, nurture, and social gesture states. If some animation clips are missing, create temporary procedural joint-driven motions for those states until proper clips are imported. I do not want agents freezing in place because an animation clip is unavailable. Missing animation should fall back gracefully to a meaningful motion approximation.

Characters should never look dead when alive. Idle itself should be rich. Add breathing, micro-sway, weight shifts, head turns, attention shifts, tiny posture resets, curiosity glances, and subtle emotional flavor. Walking should include real body rhythm: arm swing, hip motion, foot lift, turning anticipation, stop settling, speed variation, and urgency changes. Eating should include approaching the food, orienting to it, bending or kneeling or bringing it to the mouth, a chew loop, a pause, and recovery back to neutral. Building should include carrying materials, positioning near a structure point, placing motions, striking or fastening motions if relevant, stepping back to inspect, and resuming. Talking should include facing the other agent, head nods, brief hand gestures, listening pauses, and relationship-based posture changes. Sleeping should include a rest pose, reduced motion, maybe curled or lying states if supported, and a believable wake-up transition. Social attraction, conflict, fear, excitement, or pride should each have visible expression.

The animation system must be driven by AI state, not random clip spam. I want a clean bridge between the simulation brain and the visual body. For example, if hunger gets high, the agent should visually search, reorient, move with purpose toward food, gather it, consume it, and settle afterward. If social desire is high, the agent should seek nearby agents, approach, stop at proper spacing, face them, and converse with responsive body language. If energy is low, movement should slow, posture should droop, and idle behavior should become heavier. If danger is detected, posture should tighten, look direction should snap toward the stimulus, and movement should become faster or more erratic depending on personality. If reproductive or family-oriented behaviors exist, those should also have respectful and stylized behavior loops rather than awkward teleport logic.

Treat the whole thing as an artificial species simulation, not a single-purpose NPC system. These beings should be capable of the broad categories humans are capable of within the sandbox’s design: exploring, gathering, eating, resting, socializing, bonding, breeding, caring, building, crafting, learning, reacting to environment, expressing mood, competing, collaborating, defending, forming routines, and changing habits over time. That does not mean you need every full gameplay mechanic finished immediately, but the character architecture should clearly support all of it. Build the visual and animation foundation like this is going to become a true artificial life platform.

Refactor the architecture into clean parts. I want a CharacterAssetManager that loads and caches base character assets and animation clips. I want a CharacterFactory that creates character instances, applies randomized variation, sets materials, assigns accessories, and supports future species variants. I want a CharacterAnimator that wraps AnimationMixer, handles clip registration, crossfades, playback control, animation layers if needed, fallback behaviors, and state-based transitions. I want a CharacterController that handles steering, turning, acceleration, stop behavior, path following, terrain grounding, and sync between actual velocity and animation playback speed. I want a CharacterBrainAdapter that listens to needs, mood, task, memory, social intent, and world stimuli, then converts them into presentation instructions such as animation state, posture bias, attention target, emotional set, and movement urgency. Do not leave this as one chaotic update function.

Make sure movement quality improves too. I do not want instant snap rotation, ground sliding, or movement disconnected from body motion. Characters should turn before fully committing to a direction when appropriate. They should accelerate and decelerate. They should settle after stopping. Their movement style should change based on urgency, confidence, or exhaustion. Their walk cycle speed should match the actual velocity so the feet do not appear to skate. Their heads should track points of interest. Their bodies should react to nearby agents and objects. If terrain has height variation, support foot placement later, or at least maintain convincing grounding with contact shadows and vertical offsets.

Social believability matters. Agents should not overlap during conversations. They should orient toward each other, maintain spacing, shift weight while listening, sometimes gesture while speaking, and show emotional differences based on relationship state. A happy interaction should not look like a tense confrontation. Curiosity should not look like sleepiness. Attraction should not look identical to hunger. Build posture presets and emotion overlays so mood and social context influence the same base animations.

I want variation across the population. Do not clone one identical doll many times. Add height ranges, outfit colors, hair or hat variation, body shape variation within the art style, posture tendencies, idle tendencies, and personality expression biases. Some agents can stand more upright, some can be more curious, some more sluggish, some more energetic. Visual diversity matters because this is supposed to feel like a society, not a copy-paste demo.

Improve materials and presentation too. The world already has a gentle low-poly look, so characters should be lit in a way that makes them readable and attractive. Use a soft directional light with ambient fill. Add contact shadows or simple blob shadows so they sit in the world better. Make sure outfit colors separate well from the environment. You can add subtle face details, simple eye meshes or decals, light rim enhancement at certain times of day, and accessory props for jobs or roles. Keep it stylized, clean, and readable from the gameplay camera.

The world interaction should become much more visible. If an agent sees a resource, I want a look-at moment, then a decision, then a walk, then a gather or inspect motion. If they carry something, the carried item should visually travel with them. If they build, show that sequence. If they harvest, show that sequence. If they eat, show that sequence. If they rest, show that sequence. If they reproduce or nurture, create tasteful, stylized, game-appropriate behavior loops that imply the system clearly without making it awkward or crude. The whole point is that the world should visually reflect internal simulation.

Please build debugging support too. I want to be able to toggle skeleton visibility, current animation state, current AI state, current need values, current mood, current target, and path debug lines. Let me switch between placeholder and upgraded character models. Let me manually trigger animation states to test them. Let me inspect performance cost, animation counts, and distance-based throttling behavior. This needs to be easy to tune.

Performance still matters because this is a life simulator and could have many agents. Reuse assets correctly. Clone skinned meshes safely. Share materials where possible. Introduce lower-detail character variants or simpler update frequency for distant characters. Reduce animation update work for far-away agents. Use cheap substitutes for very distant agents if needed. Preserve the feel of life without melting the browser. I want the upgrade to be strong, but I also want it built like someone who understands that a simulation can scale.

I want implementation in phases. First inspect the current system and identify exactly why the models look bad, why actions are not being expressed, where movement and AI state are currently handled, and where the architecture is too messy. Then replace the character visual system with either proper imported rigged .glb assets or a strong articulated fallback generator. Then add the animation system with mixers, clips, states, and crossfades. Then connect AI state to visible behavior. Then add polish: shadows, lighting, posture overlays, emotional presets, accessory variation, carried items, and better grounding. Then optimize for many agents. Finally, leave clear notes about how to swap in even better custom species art later.

Do not just explain what should be done. Actually refactor and implement the system. Keep the simulation running while upgrading it. Keep code modular. No giant monolithic file. No vague placeholders left pretending to be finished. If an imported asset path fails, fail gracefully and fall back to the articulated procedural character system.

The end result should feel like this: a small but convincing artificial society of stylized beings living inside a believable world, where inner needs and thoughts turn into visible behavior. It should feel like the start of a real AI species sandbox. Not a tech demo. Not a student prototype. Not a walking-loop placeholder world.

Nice extras, if you can fit them in: blinking, random idle variants, tool-specific work animations, carrying items in hand, small child or elder body variations if the simulation supports age, sleeping on beds or ground, simple family grouping behavior, emotion icons or thought indicators, face expression switching, and basic inverse kinematics later for feet or hands interacting with terrain or objects.

Build it with ambition. This is not just about making characters prettier. This is about making artificial life visible.
